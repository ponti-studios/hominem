# Design

## Information Design

**First principle:** Information should be quickly digestible and true.

| Dimension     | Question                            |
| ------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| **Accuracy**  | Is it true?                         |
| **Clarity**   | Can the reader grasp it in seconds? |
| **Hierarchy** | What's most important?              |
| **Context**   | Why should the reader care?         |
| **Flow**      | What comes next?                    |

### Applications

- **Dashboards** — surface the decision, not the data.
- **Presentations** — one idea per slide; the verbal layer carries the nuance.
- **Writing** — short paragraphs, strong topic sentences, meaningful headings.
- **Product UI** — every screen should answer: "What do I do here and why?"

## Serendipity in Design

### The Discovery Problem

Hotels, movies, and TV shows are hard to pick because we don't know what we're looking for. Previously we relied on serendipity — watching whatever came on television. Now options are seemingly endless.

Satisfaction is difficult to determine and difficult to plan.

### Desires Are Contextual

Individuals believe they understand their desires but fail to grasp them until options are presented and a narrative is sold. Desire is reactive, not pre-existing.

### The Three-Episode Rule

Netflix found viewers watched 3 episodes on average before being hooked: episode 1 introduces characters and premise, episode 2 reinforces, episode 3 delivers full storyline. Humans figure out whether they like things through exposure over time.

### Implications

- The bottleneck isn't matching — it's discovery.
- Individuals can only prefer available options.
- There is no optimum way to make decisions aside from making them as quickly as possible with the best information you currently have.

## Form & Corners

The digital world rounded its corners to feel "premium" — phones became organic soap bars. But everything humans have built to store information is a rectangle: books, paintings, cinema screens, windows, legal pads, bricks.

The rounded corner is a "safe area" scam. Developers spend half their lives coding around notches and radii. Pixels are physically forbidden from showing the full frame. A movie on a modern flagship phone has a sliver of the director's vision eaten by a bezel designed to feel "ergonomic."

Sharp corners are the mark of a tool — scalpels, chisels, reference monitors. When we round the corner, we admit the phone is no longer a tool for production but a lozenge for consumption. We traded the precision of the grid for the aesthetics of the nursery.

**Key question:** Can you build a revolution on a radius?
