# Naming

## Status

The product direction is stronger than the current name.

We should treat both `Hominem` and `Hakumi` as transitional rather than final brand decisions.

## Why the current names are weak

### Hominem

`Hominem` feels like legacy infrastructure naming rather than product naming.

Problems:

- it carries the older multi-domain, life-management scope that we are actively moving away from
- it sounds formal and abstract rather than calm, clear, or personal
- it does not naturally suggest notes, context, or thought
- it feels more like a company or platform layer than a daily-use product

There is also preliminary public trademark risk. A public trademark listing shows a 2025 U.S. application for `HOMINEM` in AI-related software and SaaS.

### Hakumi

`Hakumi` is softer and more product-like than `Hominem`, but it still does not fully support the product story.

Problems:

- it is opaque and does not naturally communicate what the product does
- it depends on an origin story users will never know
- it does not clearly reinforce notes, context, memory, or continuity
- it feels pleasant, but generic

There is also preliminary public overlap risk. A technology studio is already using `HAKUMI` publicly.

## What the name needs to do

The product is a notes-first personal workspace. The name should support that.

It should feel:

- calm
- clear
- memorable
- ownable
- useful in both consumer and product writing

It should imply at least one of these ideas:

- capture
- notes
- memory
- continuity
- context
- thought
- returning to what matters

## Naming principles

### Prefer clarity over lore

The name should work even if nobody ever hears the origin story.

### Prefer warmth over grandeur

The product is intimate and daily-use. The name should feel human and lived-in, not institutional or overly ambitious.

### Prefer specificity over generic AI language

Avoid names that sound like a generic assistant, a model wrapper, or a productivity startup from the current cycle.

### Prefer semantic fit over invented exoticism

Invented words are acceptable, but only if they still feel natural to say, easy to spell, and emotionally aligned with the product.

### Prefer long-term brand room over feature naming

The product centers on notes and chat today, but the name should still work if the workspace grows into richer memory, retrieval, and action features later.

## Naming territories worth exploring

We should explore names in a few semantic directions rather than brainstorming randomly.

### Memory and recall

Names that suggest remembering, returning, resurfacing, or keeping.

Why it fits:

- strong connection to durable context
- supports the idea of a workspace that compounds over time

### Notes and thought

Names that suggest writing, thinking, drafting, reflection, or shaping ideas.

Why it fits:

- keeps notes central
- reinforces the product as a thinking tool rather than a task machine

### Place and continuity

Names that suggest one place, one home, one thread, one running record.

Why it fits:

- aligns with the one-workspace thesis
- supports calmness and coherence

### Clarity and signal

Names that suggest making sense of fragments, surfacing what matters, or seeing connections.

Why it fits:

- maps well to the role of chat and context without making AI the star

## Territories to avoid

- generic AI-assistant naming
- hyper-productive, hustle-oriented naming
- names that imply a suite of separate apps
- names that sound like finance, health, or enterprise software
- names that require explanation to be pronounceable
- names that are too similar to existing note or productivity tools

## Recommendation

We should rename.

Current recommendation:

- retire `Hominem` as a product-facing name
- treat `Hakumi` as a temporary working name only
- run a structured naming pass against the current product thesis, not the old platform vision

## Next pass

The next naming pass should produce:

1. 20 to 30 candidate names across 3 to 4 semantic territories
2. a shortlist of 5 to 7 names that best match the product
3. a lightweight public collision screen for each shortlist candidate
4. one recommended replacement name plus two backups

### Current recommendation

#### Frontrunner: Noteward

`Noteward` is the best current candidate.

Why it works:

- it keeps notes at the center
- it implies direction and movement rather than storage alone
- it feels more ownable than plain descriptive names
- it still leaves room for chat, context, and future memory features
- it matches the current product thesis better than `Hakumi` or `Hominem`

Tradeoffs:

- it is more product-descriptive than evocative
- it sounds more deliberately coined than naturally inherited

That tradeoff is acceptable for now because clarity and fit matter more than lore.

#### Backup 1: Afterkeep

`Afterkeep` is the strongest backup.

Why it works:

- it suggests what happens after capture: the thought stays kept
- it supports continuity, memory, and return
- it feels calm and durable

Tradeoffs:

- it leans archival
- it is less obviously notes-first than `Noteward`

#### Backup 2: Paperthread

`Paperthread` is the most emotionally distinctive backup.

Why it works:

- it connects writing and continuity
- it feels warm, human, and memorable
- it avoids generic AI language completely

Tradeoffs:

- it sounds more literary and brand-like than product-clear
- it may skew slightly analog or craft-oriented

### Recommendation for the next step

Move forward with this order:

1. Noteward
2. Afterkeep
3. Paperthread

If we want a name that is clearest and closest to the current thesis, choose `Noteward`.

If we want a name that is softer and more emotionally resonant, choose `Afterkeep`.

If we want a name that feels the most brand-like and distinctive, choose `Paperthread`.
